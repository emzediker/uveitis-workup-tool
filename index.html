How would you explain this diagnosis and treatment to the patient in layperson terms? (Must include diagnosis and etiology.)

“You have inflammation inside your right eye called uveitis. Based on how your eye looks and your test results, this may be related to a condition called sarcoidosis, which can cause inflammation in different parts of the body, including the lungs and eyes.

We’re treating the eye inflammation with a strong anti-inflammatory eye drop, and we’re using a second drop to rest the iris, which helps with pain and helps prevent internal sticking or scarring.

Because this may be connected to inflammation elsewhere in your body, we’re also referring you for additional medical evaluation. I want to see you back in 2–3 days to make sure the inflammation is improving and to monitor your eye pressure.

If you notice worsening vision, increasing pain, new floaters, flashing lights, or a sudden change in vision, contact us right away or seek urgent care.”

  • Toxoplasma IgG / IgM:

Diagnosis

Correct Diagnosis: Toxoplasmosis retinochoroiditis (posterior uveitis), OS

Plan

  • Therapeutic: Oral TMP-SMX (Bactrim DS) *if no sulfa allergy* OR alternative regimen per ophthalmology.
  • Dosage / Sig: Example: TMP-SMX DS 1 tab PO BID for 4–6 weeks (coordinate with ophthalmology).
  • Inflammation control: Topical steroid for anterior spillover if needed (e.g., pred acetate 1% OS QID) with close monitoring.
  • Referral: Refer to retina/uveitis specialist within 24–72 hours (posterior uveitis lesion).
  • Follow-Up: 2–3 days or per retina, then weekly until lesion stabilizes.
Note: If immunocompromised or macula-threatening lesion, urgency increases and systemic therapy is typically coordinated immediately.

Patient Explanation

Suggested Explanation:

“You have inflammation in the back part of your left eye, and we can see an active spot of infection/inflammation on the retina. This pattern is most consistent with toxoplasmosis, which is a common organism many people have been exposed to in the past, and it can sometimes reactivate inside the eye.

Because this involves the retina, treatment is usually an oral medication to control the underlying cause, and we often coordinate care with a retina or uveitis specialist quickly to protect vision. We’ll also monitor you closely over the next few days to make sure the inflammation is improving.

If you notice sudden worsening vision, increasing floaters, flashes of light, or a curtain-like shadow, contact us urgently.”
